Causes of failures

  • The flame intensity is too high.
    If the flame intensity is high relative to the speed of extrusion, the extrusion would ususally seperate into two clumps.


  • Speed of extrusion is too high
    If the speed of extrusion is high relative to the feed rate of the material, you can achieve thin extrusions. If the speed of the carriage is too high, the extruded material may not cool instantly and collapse.



  • Flame intensity is too low
    For this test, a mini Butane torch was used. The following footage was sped up by 100X. The rated heat of the flame generated by this torch is 1370ºC, too low to fully liquify the glass. Hence, the heat generated is barely enough to bend the glass rod.



  • Re-Heating and Deviation
    Without the use of vacuum to evacuate the hot air, the flame would reheat the previously extruded geometry. This causes deviation from intended extrusion path.



  • Glass suction
    If the vacuum hose is placed too close the semi-liquid glass, it may get sucked in. Maintaining an appropriate distance and angle is crucial to correct way of evacuating hot-air.

Geometry Test

Over 100 iterations were done in order to find the correct settings for extrusion. The tests included generation of:-

  • Vertical Extrusion Test
    This is one of the most primary tests to check the consistency of the extrusion in 1D. It is conducted vertically so that gravity results in minimal distortion along the extrusion path.



  • Vertical Arc Generation
    This test was used to create vertical arc geometry in order to observe any lateral sag.



  • Horizontal Circle
    There is no vertical component in this extrusion. It is used to observe any dragging of the extruded geometry away from the intended path. This test helps in reducing the relative dip in extrusion from the from the initial start point.
